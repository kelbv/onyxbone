Chapter 4: The Crimson Protocol

The revelation that millions, perhaps billions, were carriers of the ONYXBONE vectors sent tremors through the corridors of power worldwide. In the COBR, the mood was grim. The strategy of ‘Dissonant Messaging Injection,’ as outlined in Stage I of the VECTOR SKELETION Protocol, had not just failed; it had backfired, fueling paranoia and a desperate hunger for truth that official channels could no longer control.

Prime Minister Eleanor Havers convened an emergency session. The holographic display, once showing the Estonian footage, now cycled through global news feeds: riots in major cities, overwhelmed healthcare systems, and the increasingly organized online communities of the ‘Vector-positive’ demanding answers and rights.

“Stage I is a catastrophic failure,” the Defence Secretary stated bluntly, his voice echoing in the tense silence. “The public isn’t dissonant; they’re terrified and angry. We need to consider escalating our response. The protocol exists for a reason.”

Debating the Unthinkable

Dr. Fara Bashiri, her face etched with fatigue, presented her latest findings. “The vectors are… adaptive. Our initial understanding was incomplete. They’re not merely passive implants. There’s evidence of inter-vector communication, a subtle networking effect we’re only beginning to comprehend. Some carriers are reporting… shared sensations, fragmented memories that are not their own.”

This revelation cast a new, more ominous light on the situation. The term ‘assimilation,’ once a theoretical risk in the VECTOR SKELETION document, felt chillingly imminent.

Jonny Nash, ever the pragmatist, cut to the chase. “What are our options, Fara? Realistically. APEX-CORTEX? Are we seriously considering co-opting these things? Or are we looking at… Black Salt?” The final two words hung in the air, a euphemism for a societal reset so profound it was almost unimaginable.

“APEX-CORTEX, Stage II, involves integrating neurolaced operatives,” Bashiri explained, her gaze sweeping the room. “Attempting to understand and perhaps even influence the vector network from within. It’s high-risk, ethically fraught, and its success is far from guaranteed. As for Black Salt…” She paused. “That is a last resort, Prime Minister. A societal wipe. The consequences are incalculable.”

Havers listened, her expression unreadable. The weight of the decision pressed down on her. To authorize APEX-CORTEX was to delve deeper into an unknown, potentially hostile, symbiosis. To even contemplate Black Salt was to admit defeat on a scale that would redefine human history.

The Watchers

Unseen by Havers and her team, other eyes monitored the unfolding global crisis. In a sterile, windowless facility deep beneath the Nevada desert, a different kind of briefing was taking place. Here, the ONYXBONE phenomenon was not a surprise, but a long-anticipated event.

A figure known only as ‘The Director’ addressed a small, select group. “Project ONYXBONE has entered active manifestation, globally, ahead of our projected timeline. The UK’s VECTOR SKELETION protocol is, as anticipated, proving inadequate. Their internal designation ‘Black Salt’ aligns with our ‘Crimson Protocol’ – a failsafe we hoped never to deploy, but for which we must now prepare.”

On their screens, data streams flowed – not just news feeds, but bio-telemetry from select individuals worldwide, including a teenage girl in a London suburb named Maya. Her unique vector signature, the faint whispers she perceived, were of particular interest.

“The vectors are not merely a threat,” The Director continued, his voice devoid of emotion. “They are a catalyst. An evolutionary accelerant. Our objective remains unchanged: to guide the outcome, to ensure that when the new paradigm emerges, we are its architects, not its subjects.”

The Stirrings of Resistance

Meanwhile, the ‘Vector-positive’ were not waiting passively. Online communities, initially formed out of fear and confusion, were evolving. Groups like ‘Echo United’ and ‘BoneDeep’ began to organize, sharing information, offering support, and demanding transparency from governments that seemed more interested in control than care.

Maya, still reeling from her diagnosis, found a fragile sense of belonging in these digital spaces. The shared experiences, the collective search for meaning in their altered reality, provided a lifeline. The whispers in her marrow were still there, but now, she knew she wasn’t the only one hearing them.

The world was on a knife-edge. Governments weighed unthinkable choices, hidden organizations maneuvered in the shadows, and ordinary people, their very biology rewritten, began to find their voice.
